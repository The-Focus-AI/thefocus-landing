# Subject: The six weeks before any AI

A bookkeeping company wanted to automate invoice data entry. Food service clients, dozens of invoices a week, PDFs and cell phone photos and handwritten notes. Straightforward OCR problem. The models can handle it.

Except the business logic had never been written down.

Some suppliers put fuel service fees into soft costs. Others put bottle deposits there. Weight-based pricing versus unit-based pricing. Suppliers who invalidate old invoice numbers versus ones who reissue under the same number. Six weeks of work preceded any AI — not because the technology was hard, but because the business process had never been made explicit.

The humans had been absorbing ambiguity that a machine could not.

This is the first transition in what we are calling the Org Age of AI framework, and it is the hardest one: making the organization legible to itself.

Here is the uncomfortable part. A great deal of institutional knowledge has never been written down, and some of that is not accidental. When the rules live in someone's head, that person is indispensable. When the process is undocumented, nobody can question whether it makes sense. Organizational opacity protects turf.

At a construction firm, we inherited a data sync system — 224 commits from a single developer. When that developer left, the system broke. Cost code mappings lived in one person's head. "Plumbing" had been renamed to "15.1 PLUMBING" in the accounting system and only one person knew the translation.

But here is the part nobody expected: once we surfaced the data at the bookkeeping firm, fewer "exceptions" started coming through from the suppliers. As the light pushed out the darkness, fewer games were being played on the other side. Expenses that had been slipped in unnoticed stopped appearing.

Making work legible means making it inspectable, and that is a form of vulnerability. It is, on some level, a matter of trust.

The work is unglamorous. Recording meetings so they become searchable. Documenting exception rules. Cleaning data into structured formats. The output is a spreadsheet of mappings and a document that explains what terms mean. There is no demo.

**What this gets misunderstood as:** "We need an AI strategy" or "We need to pick the right tools." The blocker is not technology. It is that the company cannot describe its own workflows. This is where pilot purgatory lives — pilot after pilot, each works in isolation, none connect, nothing accumulates.

**What makes it worth doing anyway:** everything you build to make the organization legible to machines also makes it better for humans. Onboarding gets faster. Bus factor drops. The organization becomes more resilient. This is not overhead on the way to AI. It is organizational hygiene that AI forces you to finally do.
