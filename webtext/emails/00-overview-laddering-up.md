# Subject: The four transitions nobody wants to talk about

Everyone wants agents. Nobody wants to do the work that makes agents possible.

We just published a new piece in our Org Age of AI series — ["Laddering up from chatboxes to autonomy"](https://thefocus.ai/posts/laddering-up-from-chatboxes-to-autonomy/) — and it lays out the pattern we keep seeing with our clients.

A product leader watches an agent demo. It resolves support tickets end to end, or synthesizes documents into a report with a real recommendation. The demo is real. The capability exists. Then the company looks inward and realizes: processes run on habit, critical knowledge lives in three people's heads, and the data systems were built by different teams who never talked to each other.

Most companies want to skip from scattered ChatGPT use straight to autonomy. The middle layers get skipped in the planning. They are the ones that matter.

We broke it down into four transitions, each load-bearing, each built on the one before it:

1. **Making the organization legible to itself** — the hardest one. The business logic has never been written down, and some of that is not accidental.

2. **Trusting your own data** — the most underestimated one. Sounds like an engineering problem. It is a trust problem.

3. **The system starts acting on what it sees** — the most overhyped one. Everyone calls it "deploying agents." The actual difficulty is organizational.

4. **The system changes how the organization works** — the most profound one. The org has to decide whether it will let a system influence how it operates.

Over the next few weeks we will dig into each of these individually — with the real stories, the uncomfortable parts, and what we actually learned doing the work.

The thread that connects them: the work required to make an organization legible to machines is the same work that makes it better for humans. AI is the forcing function for organizational hygiene that should have been done years ago.
